**Compiled:** 2026-05-23

## Quick wins (do this month)

2. **Build a 60-account target list** — production companies, agencies, and venues that book live-event crew. Each account gets a 5-touch nurture sequence over 6 months.
3. **Set up a single inbound tracking system.** Even a Google Sheet with five columns: Date, Source, Contact, Show, Status. Without this, you're flying blind on what works.
4. **Audit pricing transparency.** SMPL's edge is that the estimate matches the invoice. Make sure every marketing surface emphasizes this — homepage, contact form, blog posts, social bios.
5. **Get the first 5 client reviews** on Google + LinkedIn (testimonial format). Email past clients you have warm relationships with; ask directly; offer a 10-minute follow-up call as gratitude.

## The buyer

The Show Masters buyer is one of four people, depending on the show:

1. **Production Manager (PM)** — Books the crew. Owns the budget line. Lives on the call sheet during the show. Repeat buyer if you treat them well.
2. **Producer / Project Manager** — Books the PM, which books the crew. Less in the weeds, more focused on outcome and budget. Sees you on the invoice but rarely on the floor.
3. **Venue Operations Director** — Books crew when the venue is selling crew as a service (corporate ballroom, mid-tier convention center). Different sales cycle — longer, more institutional.
4. **Production company owner** — The decision maker in agencies. The relationship-driven one. Books based on trust earned over years.

Each persona reads different content. PMs want operational specifics (the 12-hour load-in blog post). Producers want outcomes (case studies). Venue ops want institutional trust (insurance + certifications). Owners want shared values (the W-4 story).

**Build a 4-track funnel.** Don't try to message all four the same way.

## The booking funnel

**Awareness → Consideration → Decision → Booking → Retention**

| Stage | What the buyer is doing | What we provide | KPI |
|---|---|---|---|
| Awareness | Searching for "stagehand company Orlando" | SEO-optimized city + service pages | Organic traffic, GBP views |
| Consideration | Comparing 2-4 vendors | Case studies, blog posts, social proof | Time on site, pages per visit |
| Decision | Requesting an estimate | Fast same-day quote, transparent pricing | Estimate-to-quote response time |
| Booking | Signing contract | Easy contract, clear COI, point of contact | Estimate-to-booking conversion rate |
| Retention | Booking again | Same Crew Lead, 72-hour invoice, no surprises | Repeat-book % within 12 months |

**The current funnel's bottleneck** is at Decision → Booking. Producers who request estimates convert at a strong rate when the response is same-day. The single biggest marketing leverage point is therefore not adding more top-of-funnel — it's making sure every estimate request gets a fast, detailed response.

## Target account strategy

Build a list of 60 high-value target accounts in the first 90 days. Three categories:

### Category 1: Production companies that crew live events (~30 accounts)

Examples:
- Freeman, GES, Encore (major convention service contractors)
- ProductionGlue, Empire Entertainment, Mosaic Experiential (NYC + LA)
- Regional production houses with FL/TX presence

For each: identify the booking decision-maker, find them on LinkedIn, follow them, comment thoughtfully on their posts (not generic), DM them once after 30 days of warming with a specific, relevant insight (not a pitch). 6 months of patient nurturing converts roughly 1 in 5 of these accounts to a discovery call.

### Category 2: Agencies (~15 accounts)

Marketing/event agencies that produce client work. Different cycle — they're booking on behalf of a brand, so the relationship is with the agency PM, not the end client.

Examples:
- Big agencies with FL/TX offices (Ogilvy, Edelman, Weber Shandwick)
- Mid-size event-specialty agencies (Jack Morton, George P. Johnson, Sparks)
- Boutique experiential agencies

These are higher-volume but lower-margin. Worth building because they book repeat business at predictable cadence.

### Category 3: Venues with in-house production needs (~15 accounts)

Examples:
- Orange County Convention Center (FL)
- Gaylord Palms Resort (FL)
- Hilton Anatole (Dallas)
- George R. Brown Convention Center (Houston)
- AT&T Stadium (Arlington)
- Globe Life Field (Arlington)
- Dr. Phillips Center (Orlando)
- Amway Center (Orlando)

These have lower marketing-receptivity (institutional buyers) but higher-trust outcomes (preferred-vendor relationships once earned). Worth a slower, more patient cultivation.

## Outreach cadence

For a target-account contact, the recommended sequence:

- **Day 0:** Connect on LinkedIn with a personalized note referencing their work
- **Day 14:** Engage publicly on a piece of their content (comment, not just like)
- **Day 30:** Send a relevant article (one of our blog posts, or a non-SMPL industry piece). No pitch.
- **Day 60:** Direct ask for a discovery call. Keep it short — "Producer to producer, would love 20 minutes."
- **Day 90:** Final follow-up if no response. After that, drop to a quarterly check-in.

Conversion rate at scale: 5-15% of contacts will agree to a discovery call by Day 90. Of those, 30-50% will book within 12 months. So 60 contacts → 3-9 discovery calls → 1-4 first bookings. Expect a 6-12 month cycle on enterprise accounts.

## Reviews playbook

Reviews are the single largest local-SEO + decision-stage trust factor. Treat them like a product.

### Generating reviews

After every show, email the producer with three things:
1. The invoice (boring, expected)
2. A thank-you note from the Crew Lead (specific, memorable)
3. A direct ask: "If we did good work, would you mind leaving a review on Google?" with the direct link

**Conversion rate** of this ask: 20-30%. Across 30 shows/month, that's 6-10 reviews. Over a year, 70-120 reviews.

### Responding to reviews

Every review gets a response within 48 hours. Templates:

- **5-star:** "Thanks [Name], it was a pleasure crewing your [show type] at [venue]. [Specific thing the team did]. Looking forward to the next one."
- **3-4-star:** "[Name], thank you for the feedback. We hear you on [specific issue]. We've already changed [specific thing]. If you'd be open to a quick call about your next show, here's my direct: [phone]."
- **1-2-star:** Reach out privately first (Crew Lead or owner), resolve offline, then respond publicly: "[Name], we've spoken about this and we're putting [specific action] in place. Thank you for the candor — it makes us better."

**Never respond defensively.** Never argue the facts publicly even when you're right.

## Pricing positioning

SMPL's pricing edge is **transparency**, not lowest price. Don't get drawn into bid-shopping. The marketing message should reinforce:

- All-in hourly rate, no admin percentages
- 72-hour invoice with the same number as the estimate
- W-4 employees with workers' comp + GL + payroll taxes baked in
- COI on request, no upcharge

When a producer asks "can you go lower?" the answer is usually "we can adjust the crew sheet, but the rate per role is set — that's the rate that includes [list]." Producers who continue to push for lower rate are not the right fit; let them go to a 1099 vendor and learn the lesson on someone else's invoice.

## Email marketing

Beyond the monthly newsletter (covered in `02-Social-Media.md`), the high-leverage email work:

### Transactional emails

- **Estimate response** — Same-day, with a phone number for the producer if they want to talk. Treat this as a sales conversation, not a quote.
- **Booking confirmation** — Specific Crew Lead named, with their direct line.
- **Crew Lead pre-show note** — 48 hours before load-in, the Crew Lead emails the producer directly. Builds the personal relationship that drives repeat business.
- **Invoice + thank-you** — Within 72 hours of load-out. Includes the review ask.
- **Post-show NPS** — One question: "Would you book us again?" If 9-10, ask for a review. If 7-8, ask for feedback. If 0-6, ask for a phone call.

### Lifecycle emails

- **Quarterly check-in** with past clients who haven't booked in 6 months
- **Show-season prep** emails to past clients 60 days before their typical busy season
- **Cancellation/postponement** support emails — sympathy + flexibility, often becomes a relationship-deepening moment

## Reporting cadence

A monthly one-page marketing report should answer:

1. How many new producer leads came in this month? From what source?
2. How many estimates did we send? How many converted?
3. What's the average revenue per new client? Per repeat client?
4. What's the repeat-book rate over 12 months? (This is the most important number.)
5. What's the next action item (one specific thing)?

The repeat-book rate is the most important metric. A high-repeat-book business compounds; a low-repeat-book business stays expensive on customer acquisition forever.

## Budget benchmarks

For a $5-10M annual revenue service business, healthy marketing spend lands at 4-8% of revenue. For SMPL at that scale:

- Tooling: $100-200/mo
- Content / contractor labor: $1,500-3,500/mo (one part-time content/social person, or one full-time at the high end)
- Paid ads (if any): $500-2,500/mo (mostly Google Ads on high-intent local terms)
- Earned media / PR contractor: $0-2,000/mo (only if scaling)
- Reviews + reputation tooling: $50-200/mo (Birdeye, Podium, or similar)

Total: $2k-8k/mo depending on stage. At $4k/mo, you should expect 3-8 new producer accounts per year directly attributable to marketing, on top of organic word-of-mouth growth.

## What NOT to do

- **Don't run paid Facebook/Instagram ads on cold audiences.** B2B services with $30k+ contract values don't convert through Meta-ads-style impulse buys.
- **Don't pay for "leads" from lead-gen networks** (Thumbtack, Bark, etc.). The leads are typically poor quality and shared with 10+ competitors.
- **Don't redo the brand identity.** The current SMPL brand (dark + gold, serif italic accents, JetBrains Mono labels) is distinctive and well-executed. Keep it.
- **Don't expand to a new vertical before the existing one is healthy.** Stay focused on live-event crew + I&D. Don't add weddings, don't add corporate AV rentals (different sales motion entirely).
- **Don't outsource the brand voice.** The voice on the current site is direct, specific, operator-tone. A typical agency will sand that down into generic SaaS-ese. Keep the voice in-house.
