# 10 — Pricing Notes: Competitor Rate Intel + Defending the SMPL Story

How we hold our price when a producer says "I can get it cheaper." Quick answer: we don't compete on price — we compete on what's baked into it. This doc gives the operator + sales team the exact lines to use when the pricing conversation comes up.

## The pricing defense — when a producer says "you're more expensive"

This is the conversation. Memorize this.

### Step 1 — Don't get defensive. Get specific.

> "Sure, our line items look higher. Want me to walk through what's actually in the rate? Three minutes."

If yes, walk through Step 2. If no, send the printed comparison sheet (template below) and follow up in 24 hours.

### Step 2 — The three-line breakdown

> "When you book stagehand labor at $40/hr from a 1099 shop, you're paying:
>
> 1. **$40 for the hand.**
> 2. **Roughly $4–$6/hr in additional workers' comp insurance you'll have to carry** — because contractors aren't covered under their own employer's policy, and if someone gets hurt on your show, your event policy is on the hook.
> 3. **Plus the legal exposure of having 1099 labor on a venue floor.** Texas and Florida labor laws are aggressive on misclassification.
>
> When you book the same role from SMPL at $48/hr, all of that is already in. No surprise insurance bill, no contractor classification risk, no admin fee bolted on at the end."

### Step 3 — The math reframe

> "Run the numbers on a 100-hour show. At 1099, you're at $4,000 + ~$500 in insurance gap + roughly $250 in admin fee = ~$4,750. With us, same 100 hours = $4,800 total. We're $50 more — for predictability, insurance certainty, and the same Crew Lead every time you book us."

### Step 4 — The trust card

> "Honestly — if you're shopping purely on the hourly rate, we're not the right fit. We're for producers who've been burned by 1099 labor before and never want to do it again."

That last line is the closer. Producers who haven't been burned will go with the cheaper option. The ones who have stay loyal forever.

A one-page PDF the project lead emails after the rate conversation. Keep it factual; let the math do the talking.

**Title:** "Comparing apples to apples: what's actually in your stagehand rate"

**Rows:**
- Hourly labor: $X / $Y

- Crew Lead per shift: usually +$80–$120 / **always included**
- Same crew next show: not guaranteed / **yes**
- Detailed invoice within 72 hours: variable / **yes, every time**
- W-2 misclassification risk: yours / **none**

Sub-total + true-cost summary at bottom.

## When to discount

Almost never. But specific exceptions:

| Situation | Discount | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Volume commit (10+ shows / year, contracted) | 5–8% off rate | We get predictability; they get a price |
| Multi-day show (3+ consecutive days, same crew) | $50/day Crew Lead waiver | The Crew Lead becomes more efficient day-over-day |
| Show booked 60+ days out | $0 — instead, lock the calendar | Discount the future; protect today's margin |
| Off-season (Jan, July, mid-Aug) | 5% off, only if needed to close | Otherwise we drift down the market |
| Charity / non-profit | 10–15%, on case basis | Operator decision per request |
| First-time producer testing us | Never. | If we discount the first show, every show is discounted forever. Decline politely. |

## When to walk away

We walk away from these:

1. **Race-to-bottom RFPs** — "lowest bid wins, no scoring on quality." We're not for sale at that price.
2. **Producer with reputation for non-payment** — credit-check the company, look at BBB complaints.
3. **Shows where the producer expects 1099 labor and isn't willing to switch.** This is a misclassification trap we won't enter.
4. **Single-show jobs under 12 crew-hours** — our overhead doesn't fit.
5. **Out-of-state shows where the producer won't cover travel + per diem.** Math doesn't work.

Walking away in a polite, on-brand way:

> "Appreciate you reaching out. Given the scope, we're not the right fit for this one — but if your next show is a fit for our model, I'd love to be on the short-list. Best of luck with [Event]."

That phrasing keeps the door open for next time without burning bridges.

## How to set rates over time

Review pricing quarterly. The decision rules:

1. **If win rate on estimates is above 50%**, we're priced too low — raise top of band by 3–5%.
2. **If win rate is under 30%**, either the brief isn't qualified (fix outreach) or rates are too high (small cut on bottom of band).
3. **Inflation pass**: annual minimum +3–4% to all rates each January, no exceptions. Communicate to repeat clients in Q4 of the prior year.

The single biggest pricing mistake we can make is leaving rates flat for years. Costs go up; rates must too. Producers expect it.

## What we never quote

- A flat per-show price (always per-role per-hour).
- A range without a fixed number ("$5–7k" — be specific or don't quote).
- A rate that doesn't include workers' comp + GL (this is the brand promise).
- A rate that includes a "we'll figure it out" line item.

Every estimate is line-by-line, role-by-role, hour-by-hour. Every line either has a price next to it or it's not on the estimate.
