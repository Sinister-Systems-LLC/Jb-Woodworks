# 07 — Outreach: 100 Producers in 90 Days

Outbound is how we win the first big block of new accounts without waiting on SEO to mature. The math is simple — 100 cold contacts × 8% reply rate × 25% reply-to-meeting = 2 closed accounts per 100 outreach. Repeat the cycle quarterly.

This is a sprint, not a forever job. Do it for 90 days, then evaluate.

---

## The target

100 production companies — 50 Florida, 50 Texas — split as follows:

**Florida 50:**
- 20 corporate-event producers (Orlando, Tampa, Miami)
- 10 concert / festival promoters
- 5 trade-show production firms
- 5 sports-event organizers
- 5 house-of-worship A/V firms
- 5 wedding / private-event production studios

**Texas 50:**
- 20 corporate-event producers (Dallas, Houston, Austin, San Antonio)
- 10 concert / festival promoters
- 5 trade-show production firms
- 5 sports-event organizers (the Rangers + Cowboys + Stars + Mavericks ecosystems)
- 5 house-of-worship A/V firms
- 5 wedding / private-event production studios

Build the list in a Google Sheet. Columns: Company, City, Vertical, Decision-maker name, Title, Email, LinkedIn URL, Phone, Last contact, Next touch, Status, Notes.

---

## Where to find names (in priority order)

1. **BizBash supplier directory** (free). Search by city + service type.
2. **ProductionHub.com** (free). Same.
3. **LinkedIn Sales Navigator** (free trial, then $99/mo for 1 seat). Search by "Title: producer OR production manager" + Geography + Company size 11–200.
4. **Apollo.io free tier**. Same as Sales Navigator but cheaper. ~$0–49/mo.
5. **Google searches**: `"corporate event production" "orlando" -careers -indeed -linkedin/jobs`
6. **Industry trade lists**: Live Design Online's annual supplier feature. BizBash's "Top 100 Producers" piece.
7. **Existing client referrals**. Ask 5 happy clients: "Who else in your network produces shows like this?" Get 2–3 names from each.

Goal: 25 new entries per week for 4 weeks → 100-name list by end of month 1.

---

## The 4-touch sequence

Each producer gets 4 touches over 14 days. Mix channels. Don't repeat the same email.

| Touch | Day | Channel | What to say |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Day 1 | Email (short) | The pitch (see below) |
| 2 | Day 4 | LinkedIn connection request + 1-line note | "Sent you a note re: SMPL stagehands earlier — would love to be a resource on FL/TX shows" |
| 3 | Day 9 | Email (different angle) | The case study or stat |
| 4 | Day 14 | Phone call | "Just wanted to make sure my note hit your inbox" |

After Day 14, mark "no reply" and move them to a 90-day re-touch queue. Don't burn them out.

---

## Email Touch 1 — the cold open

Subject: `Stagehands + riggers for [their typical city] shows`

Body:

> Hi [Name],
>
> Saw you produced [specific recent show or type of show — Google their company] — congrats on that. I'm with Show Masters Production Logistics (SMPL). We staff live-event crew — stagehands, riggers, technicians, lift operators — out of Orlando and Dallas, and have done 1,700+ shows since 2002.
>
> Three reasons producers move their labor over to us:
>
> 1. Our crew is W-4 employees, not 1099 — your liability stays clean.
> 2. Workers' comp + GL insurance are baked into the rate. No hidden line items.
> 3. You get the same Crew Lead every show — they learn your patterns.
>
> Worth a 15-minute call to see if there's a fit on your next [their typical event type]?
>
> [Your name]
> SMPL · (877) 765-2267 · showmasters.com

Notes:
- Personalize the first sentence. Generic "saw your website" gets deleted.
- Three bullet points only. More is less.
- One CTA. "Worth a 15-minute call" beats "let me know" or "happy to discuss further."
- Send between 7:30am and 10:00am the producer's local time. Higher reply rate.

## Email Touch 3 — the second angle

Send 5 days after touch 1 if no reply. Don't reference the first email.

Subject: `Quick numbers on FL/TX event labor`

Body:

> Hi [Name],
>
> Quick one. Most producers I talk to don't realize that a 1099-contractor crew is typically 18–25% cheaper *on paper* but 35–60% more expensive once you add workers' comp insurance the producer has to carry, plus the legal exposure of a contractor injury on a venue floor.
>
> At SMPL, we run W-4 crew with our insurance, baked in. No surprises, no riders, no "we forgot to mention that."
>
> If you're booking any FL or TX labor in the next 90 days, would value a 15-minute call — I'll walk you through how the rate math actually works.
>
> [Your name]
> SMPL · (877) 765-2267

---

## LinkedIn Touch 2 — the connection

Send 3 days after email 1. Add a 200-character note when sending the connection request:

> Hi [Name] — sent you a note about SMPL stagehands a few days ago. Want to be a resource for FL/TX event labor whenever it's useful. Connecting in case it helps later.

That's it. Don't pitch in the LinkedIn note. The goal is to be in their connection graph so the next pitch isn't cold.

---

## Phone Touch 4 — the close

Day 14. Pick up the phone. Use this script if you reach them; voicemail script if not.

**Live call:**

> Hi [Name], this is [Your name] from Show Masters Production Logistics. I sent you a note last week about our stagehand and rigging crew out of Orlando and Dallas — wanted to make sure it hit your inbox. Got 60 seconds?

If yes:

> Three things real quick. We're a W-4 shop — no 1099, no contractor liability for you. Our rates have workers' comp and GL baked in, so the line items on your estimate are the line items on your invoice. And the Crew Lead you book on Tuesday is the Crew Lead you get every time you book us. Worth 15 minutes to sketch how that would look on your next show?

If voicemail:

> Hi [Name], [Your name] from SMPL Show Masters — left a note earlier. We staff stagehands and riggers out of Orlando and Dallas, all W-4, all insured. Would love 15 minutes whenever you have a show on the calendar. Reach me at (877) 765-2267. Thanks.

## Tracking

Update the Sheet every Friday. KPIs:

- **Sends per week**: target 25 emails out, 25 LinkedIn requests, 10–15 calls.
- **Open rate**: target 45%+ (good subject lines).
- **Reply rate**: target 8%+ (industry-good for cold).
- **Meeting-booked rate**: target 25% of repliers.
- **Close rate**: target 25% of meetings.

Math: 100 contacts × 8% × 25% × 25% = 0.5% close. Multiply by 4 cycles a year = 2% annual close on cold. That's ~2 new accounts per 100 contacts per cycle, ~8 per year. Each new account is worth ~$20–$80k/year. The ROI is obvious.

---

## Tools

- **Spreadsheet**: Google Sheets (free).
- **Email-sending**: Gmail manually (best deliverability) or Apollo's sequencer ($49/mo). Avoid bulk tools — they kill deliverability.
- **LinkedIn**: Sales Navigator trial → cancel after 30 days or keep at $99/mo if it pays for itself.
- **Phone**: just the office phone. Producers hate "from a number I don't recognize" — but they hate "from a fake-looking VoIP" more.

---

## Anti-patterns (the things that destroy this funnel)

1. **Mass-blast emails.** 500 generic sends → 50 unsubscribes → reputation tanked. Better: 25 hand-personalized.
2. **Following up too fast** (next-day pestering). Two touches in 14 days. Pace it.
3. **Pitching the product before identifying the problem.** Lead with their world ("you produced X"), not ours ("we do Y").
4. **Saying "synergy" or "circle back."** Use producer's language: load-in, doors, strike, run sheet, hands.
5. **Asking for a meeting in touch 1.** Better: "Worth 15 minutes?" Lower ask, higher conversion.

---

## What to do with the names that DON'T close

After 4 touches with no reply, move them to a 90-day cooldown bucket. Re-touch in Q+1 with a different angle:

- Cycle 2 (Q2): a stat-driven email ("Florida event labor costs rose 12% in Q1 — here's what changed").
- Cycle 3 (Q3): a thought-leadership reference ("Saw our piece in [trade publication] — thought of your team").
- Cycle 4 (Q4): a referral-soft ("Working with [shared client] this fall — they thought we should connect").

A cold contact often becomes a customer in cycle 3 or 4, not cycle 1. Patience compounds.
