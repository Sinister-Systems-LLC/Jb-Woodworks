# 05 — Competitors: Who We're Up Against

A short, honest read of the other crewing companies producers might call before they call SMPL. The point isn't to bad-mouth anyone — it's to know exactly where we win, where we lose, and how to frame the pitch.

This list is the starting set. Refresh it every quarter — companies merge, fold, and rebrand constantly in the staging world.

---

## Florida competitors

### Crew One Productions (Atlanta-based, FL coverage)
**What they do well:** Big brand recognition across the Southeast. Strong rigger reputation. Have been around 25+ years.
**Where we win:** They're an Atlanta company with FL coverage; we're a Florida company with FL crew on payroll. When a producer wants "Orlando hands who already know OCCC," that's us. They also lean heavily on contractors (1099) — we don't.
**Pitch line:** "We're locally based, our crew is W-4, and we don't sub out to Atlanta when Orlando gets busy."

### IATSE Local 631 (Orlando + Central Florida)
**What they do well:** Union shop. Massive trained workforce. Required on many union shows.
**Where we win:** Non-union flexibility — corporate, private, festival, trade show work where the producer doesn't need or want union rates. Plus we're typically 15–25% cheaper for the same skillset.
**Pitch line:** "Union when you need it. Cost-efficient when you don't."

### Encore Global / PSAV (Orlando ops)
**What they do well:** Hotel in-house. Convenient if the show is at a Marriott / Hyatt / Hilton property they have a contract with.
**Where we win:** Independence from any hotel. We work all venues, we don't push captive pricing. Producers using us at OCCC keep options open across the property.
**Pitch line:** "We work every venue, not just the ones with a contract."

### Crew Connect / KBA Documentary (FL traveling labor)
**What they do well:** Aggressive on price for medium-skill labor.
**Where we win:** Quality of crew. They scrape labor boards; we hire and retain. Their I-9 turnover is high. Our 15-year crew has the muscle memory.
**Pitch line:** "Cheaper labor is the most expensive labor — when half the crew has never seen the venue."

### Local FL gig boards / Facebook groups (DIY labor)
**What they do well:** Cheap.
**Where we win:** Insurance + payroll + accountability. A producer who books off Facebook is the one liable when someone gets hurt. We bake worker's comp + GL into the rate.
**Pitch line:** "If the labor isn't insured, you are."

## Texas competitors

### Onstage Systems (Dallas)
**What they do well:** Local Dallas reputation, deep rigging chops.
**Where we win:** Statewide coverage from Dallas — we crew Houston, Austin, San Antonio without a regional surcharge. Also: traveling crew that has done 33-state shows.
**Pitch line:** "From Dallas we cover the whole Texas triangle without a markup."

### Mountain Productions (touring, TX coverage)
**What they do well:** Large national footprint. Strong stagehands.
**Where we win:** Local roots. We're not just sending crew on the road — we're the people who already know Dickies Arena, the Will Rogers complex, Globe Life Field's load-in patterns.
**Pitch line:** "Our crew didn't just fly in last night."

### Stagecall.com / Set Talent (general labor)
**What they do well:** App-based booking, ultra-fast.
**Where we win:** They're a marketplace, not a crew. Producers using them get whoever signs up that day. We're an employer — same Crew Lead, same go-to riggers, every show.
**Pitch line:** "Same crew next year, not a new app-roster."

### Local TX rigging shops (single-discipline)
**What they do well:** Specialist riggers.
**Where we win:** One-stop. Producers don't want to call 4 vendors. We staff riggers, stagehands, AV techs, lift operators, crew leads — under one contract.
**Pitch line:** "One contract, one invoice, one number to call when something changes at 2 a.m."

## The honest losses

We lose to these in specific situations. Don't pretend otherwise.

| We lose when... | Why | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| The show is union-mandatory | We're non-union (Local 631 wins) | Refer the union work, stay the producer's call for everything else |
| The show is at a captive-venue hotel | In-house AV has the contract baked in | Pitch the producer to put up a fight at contract renewal; meanwhile, win the non-captive piece |
| The producer is shopping on price alone | Race-to-bottom doesn't fit our model | Decline politely; they'll be back after a bad show |
| It's a one-off micro-show, <2 crew | Our overhead doesn't fit a 6-hour gig with 1 hand | Refer to a freelance dispatch; ask for the reciprocal referral when they have a real show |

## The non-negotiable positioning

When a producer is comparing SMPL to anyone above, lead with these three things in this order:

1. **"Our crew is W-4 employees, not 1099 contractors."** Everyone else is on contractors. The producer's liability exposure on a contractor injury is enormous. This single sentence wins half our deals.

2. **"Workers' comp and general liability are baked into the rate."** No "10% admin fee." No "insurance is a separate line." It's just in. This is the second half of our deals.

3. **"You get the same Crew Lead every time you book us."** Continuity. They learn the show. The show learns them. This is what we sell.

Everything else — pricing, location, response time — is table stakes. Those three sentences are the moat.

## Quarterly competitor research checklist

Once a quarter, spend 60 minutes doing this for each major competitor:

1. **GMB profile audit** — review count, photos, categories, recent posts.
2. **Website check** — any new locations, new services, new pricing language?
3. **LinkedIn company page** — headcount change (Sales Navigator shows this), new leadership posts?
4. **Job board scrape** — what are they hiring for? (Tells you their growth direction.)
5. **Industry mentions** — Google `"<competitor>" 2026` — any press, awards, big-show announcements?

Capture findings in a one-line note in this file, dated. Knowing the field stays cheap if you keep it up.
