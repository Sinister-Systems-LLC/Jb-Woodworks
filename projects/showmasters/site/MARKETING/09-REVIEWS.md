# 09 — Reviews: How to Build to 100+ on Each Profile

Reviews are the single highest-leverage thing we can do. Every new 5-star review on Google moves the local pack ranking AND moves a wavering buyer over the line. Most competitors have under 20 reviews. Getting to 100 — with a 4.8 average — is a moat that takes years for anyone to catch.

Target: 30 reviews per profile in 90 days. 100 per profile in 12 months. 4.8+ average maintained.

---

## The math nobody wants to hear

- About 1 in 3 happy clients leaves a review when asked. ~10% leave one unprompted.
- The window where a producer will say yes is 24 hours post-show. After 72 hours, the ask conversion drops below 10%.
- Every 10 new reviews moves the GMB ranking 0.5–1.5 positions in our market (highly competitive).
- An average rating below 4.5 starts to hurt conversion rate from search visits.

Translation: if we ask 90 clients in 90 days, ~30 will leave reviews. That gets us to target.

---

## The script (text message)

Use this exact phrasing. It outperforms longer pitches.

> Hey [Name] — great working with you on the [Event] this weekend. If you have 60 seconds, could you leave us a quick Google review? [LINK]
>
> Means everything to our crew. Thanks.
>
> — SMPL

Notes on why this works:
- "60 seconds" sets expectation that it's quick (they actually take 90 seconds — but the framing matters).
- "Means everything to our crew" makes it about the team, not the company. Activates social good.
- Direct link, no extra clicks.
- Signed informal (no "Regards" or "Sincerely"). Producers are texting humans, not getting form letters.

## When to ask

Within 24 hours of load-out. Past 72 hours, conversion drops sharply.

Best windows by producer type:

- **Corporate event producer:** day after the event, between 10am and 2pm (they're back at the desk catching up).
- **Festival / concert promoter:** day after, between 11am and 3pm.
- **Trade-show I&D client:** the morning of the next day, before they fly home.
- **House of worship lead:** Monday morning (post-Sunday).

---

## Who asks

The person who was the producer's primary point of contact during the show. NOT a generic "marketing" address. Personal connection lifts conversion 3×.

Typically the Crew Lead OR the project lead OR the operator (depending on the account tier).

---

## Where the review link comes from

GBP dashboard → "Get more reviews" → copy the short URL. Looks like:

`https://g.page/r/CXyz123abc456/review`

Pin this link in the team's Slack / WhatsApp so anyone asking has it at hand. There are two links — one per profile (Orlando and Dallas). Use the one matching where the show was geographically; if mixed (event in Texas with FL-based producer), use the office the producer's main contact runs out of.

---

## Responding to reviews

Respond to every review within 24 hours. Yes, even 5-stars. Especially 5-stars (it gets seen by future review-readers and signals we care).

### 5-star response template

> Thanks for the kind words, [Name] — really enjoyed crewing [Event] with you. Looking forward to the next one.
>
> — [Your name], SMPL

### 4-star response template

> Thanks for the feedback, [Name]. Glad most of [Event] worked smoothly — we'd love to know what we could have done better. [Your name] is reaching out directly to walk through it.
>
> — SMPL

Then follow up offline. Resolve the issue. Ask if they'd revisit the rating after.

### 3-star or worse response template

> Hi [Name] — thank you for the honest feedback. We take this seriously. [Your name] from our team will reach out today to understand what went wrong on [Event] and how we make it right.
>
> — SMPL

Then call them within 4 hours. Get the full story. Fix what you can. NEVER argue publicly. NEVER ask them to remove the review unless they offer.

### 1- or 2-star, suspected unfair / fake

If a review is from a non-customer, or refers to a show we didn't crew, or violates Google's policies (profanity, off-topic, conflict of interest):

1. Respond once, briefly, factually: "We don't have a record of working with you on [event mentioned]. Could you reach out to Orders@ShowMasters.com so we can verify?"
2. Flag the review via GBP → "Report review" → choose policy violation.
3. If Google doesn't remove it, leave it. One bad review among 50 good ones is invisible.

---

## What we DO NOT do

1. **Pay for reviews.** Illegal under FTC, and Google permanently suspends profiles caught doing this.
2. **Trade reviews with other businesses.** Same.
3. **Gate reviews ("only leave one if it's 5 stars").** Specifically illegal under FTC guidelines.
4. **Ask multiple times.** Once is enough. If they don't leave one, they don't.
5. **Use review-gating software.** "Take a 1-question survey first — if 5 stars, show Google link; if not, show feedback form." This is gating and is illegal.
6. **Review our own profile from any team member's account.** Permanent suspension.
7. **Geo-ask reviews from inside the office.** Google detects clusters and flags them.

## How to ask without it feeling awkward

The crew lead is the best asker because the producer just spent two days seeing them deliver. A natural script during load-out wrap:

> "Hey, before you go — really enjoyed running [show] with you. If you have a minute when you're back at the desk tomorrow, would you mind dropping us a Google review? It honestly helps our crew get more work. I'll send the link by text in the morning."

That's it. ~60% say "of course" verbally. ~40% follow through.

---

## Boosting the boring ones

After the first 30 reviews, growth slows. Add these mechanics:

1. **Quarterly all-client email** with the review link in the P.S. Don't pitch; just remind.
2. **Site widget**: a small "Reviews" badge in the footer linking directly to the GBP profile. Drives 1–2 reviews/month organically.
3. **Email signature**: every team email signature has the review link. "Loved working with us? [Review on Google]"
4. **At-show signage**: A simple printed QR-code card in road cases. "Liked the crew? Scan to review." Don't print on company swag — keep it light.

## The compounding payoff

The 100th review doesn't matter as much as the velocity of getting there. Google rewards consistent monthly review additions:

- 1 review/month = invisible.
- 4 reviews/month = noticeable lift in local pack ranking after 90 days.
- 8 reviews/month = top-3 local pack lock-in within 6 months (in a moderately competitive market).
- 15 reviews/month = dominant position; competitors can't catch up without years of work.

Target: average 8 new reviews per profile per month for the first year.
