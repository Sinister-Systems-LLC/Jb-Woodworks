The fantasy shop has 2,000 square feet of conditioned space, three-phase power, and room to walk a full sheet of plywood in any direction. The real shop is a one-car garage — roughly 12 by 20 feet, 240 square feet — with a standard 100-amp service, one 20-amp circuit per wall, and a concrete floor that slopes toward a drain.

Here is how to make that work for furniture-grade production without compromising on capability.

## The geometry constraint nobody talks about

Every machine in a shop has a **machine footprint** and a **work envelope** — the floor space the machine itself occupies, and the space needed to maneuver the workpiece through it. The work envelope is usually much larger than the machine footprint and is the number that actually drives layout.

A 10-inch table saw has a footprint of roughly 3 by 5 feet. To rip a 96-inch board on that saw, you need 8 feet of infeed space and 8 feet of outfeed space — adding 16 linear feet to the 5-foot machine depth. In a 20-foot garage, the table saw work envelope along that axis is 21 feet. It exceeds the room.

The solution is **overlapping work envelopes**. The infeed zone for the table saw runs through the open garage door (or into an adjacent work area). The outfeed zone runs through where the miter saw station sits when not in use. No machine needs its full work envelope clear at the same time — you are making one cut at a time.

Mapping overlapping work envelopes before you buy machines (or before you move them) is the single highest-leverage layout decision you can make.

## The anchor machine determines everything

Pick one machine as the anchor — the one that is hardest to move and has the most demanding work envelope — and position it first. In a furniture shop this is almost always the table saw.

The table saw should be in the center of the garage lengthwise, positioned so its infeed axis aligns with the garage door opening. This gives you unlimited infeed length (you can push a 12-foot slab into the garage from outside), and the outfeed runs toward the interior where you can add a roller stand or table.

The table saw fence side (right side for most saws) needs unobstructed floor space equal to the width of the widest panel you plan to rip — 48 inches for a standard sheet. Wall-mounted storage on the right wall needs to be raised above that height or kept off that wall entirely.

## Stationary machine placement priorities

**Jointer**: typically the second most space-demanding machine in a furniture shop. Position it perpendicular to the table saw if space allows, or parallel with its work envelope aimed toward an open wall or the garage door. The jointer fence side needs 6 feet of clearance for long boards.

**Planer**: the planer work envelope is deep but narrow — the board exits in one axis only. Most benchtop planers can be positioned near a wall if the outfeed side aims at open floor. Rolling it on a mobile base when in use is a workable compromise in tight shops.

**Bandsaw**: the most compact large stationary machine in terms of work envelope. The bandsaw feeds from the front and exits from the front (curves can turn in place). It can be positioned in a corner. This makes it an excellent candidate for the far corner of the garage away from the door.

**Drill press**: minimal work envelope. Can go almost anywhere; typically wall-adjacent works well.

## Mobile base strategy

In a shop this size, mobile bases are not optional — they are how you reclaim work envelope when a machine is not in use. Every stationary machine should be on a mobile base or at minimum on locking swivel casters.

The operational pattern is: roll the machine you need to its working position (clearing its work envelope), lock the casters, do the work, unlock and roll back to storage position. In a well-designed small shop this takes 30 seconds and becomes automatic.

The machines that benefit most from mobile bases: the jointer (long work envelope, positioned center-room when in use), the planer (moved to face a wall when not in use to reclaim center floor space), and the bandsaw.

The table saw is the exception — it is the anchor and typically stays in position. If you must move it, a mobile base works but adds complexity to table height and fence alignment.

## Electrical planning

A one-car garage with 100-amp service can run a furniture shop if you are thoughtful about load sequencing. You cannot run the table saw and the dust collector and the compressor simultaneously on a single 20-amp circuit, but you can run each one separately.

The practical rule: each major machine (table saw, jointer, planer, bandsaw) should have its own 20-amp 240-volt circuit. A 3-horsepower table saw running on a 15-amp 120-volt circuit will trip the breaker in cold wood — this is not a layout issue, it is an electrical one, and it needs to be solved before anything else.

Most one-car garages have one or two 20-amp 120-volt circuits. Upgrading to three or four 20-amp 240-volt circuits is a $400–$600 electrical job if your main panel has the capacity. Do it before you finalize machine placement, because circuit locations drive machine locations.

## Wall storage and vertical space

At 240 square feet, floor space is the premium resource. Wall and ceiling space is abundant by comparison.

**French cleat walls** on all four garage walls give you reconfigurable tool storage without fixed cabinet depth eating into floor clearance. A 3/4-inch plywood cleat system installed floor-to-ceiling turns every wall into programmable storage: hand tool holders, router bit trays, clamp racks, finishing supply shelves — all repositionable without fasteners.

**Overhead storage**: the garage ceiling (typically 8–9 feet in a residential garage) has room for a ceiling-hung sheet goods rack — 2-by-4 rails hung on threaded rod from the ceiling joists, accessible by tilting sheets out from overhead storage. This keeps 4-by-8 sheets off the floor and out of the work envelope without taking a square foot of wall space.

**Lumber storage**: the most efficient small-shop lumber storage is a wall-mounted cantilever rack — horizontal arms sticking out from the wall, lumber laid flat on the arms. This can be installed above the garage door when it is open (dead space otherwise) or along a long wall above machine height.

## The shop JB Woodworks started in

Our first production space was a 14-by-22 garage. Table saw anchored center, jointer on the left long wall on a mobile base, bandsaw in the back right corner, planer on a rolling cart against the back wall. Lumber storage was overhead on ceiling rails. Hand tools on a French cleat wall on the right side.

That arrangement handled full furniture production — dining tables, chairs, case pieces — for three years. What forced us out eventually was not capability, it was the inability to do large-format finishing (spray lacquer requires more ventilation than a single-door garage can provide safely) and the lack of conditioned space for sensitive wood acclimation.

The lesson: a small shop is a layout problem, not a capability problem. Solve the layout and the shop will produce.

## Checklist before finalizing your layout

- Map every machine footprint and work envelope on graph paper (1 square = 1 foot) before moving anything heavy.
- Confirm electrical circuit locations and capacities for each machine position.
- Identify which work envelopes overlap and which need to be clear simultaneously.
- Place the anchor machine first, other machines around it.
- Every stationary machine on a mobile base or casters.
- At least one clear 4-by-8-foot assembly space near the center of the shop (even if shared with another work envelope).
- Dust collector positioned for shortest run to the highest-volume producer (usually the table saw).

The shop does not need to be big. It needs to be organized around the geometry of the work.
