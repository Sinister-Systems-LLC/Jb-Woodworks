Choosing the right wood is not a matter of taste — it is a material engineering decision that determines whether a furniture piece handles daily use for 20 years or 200. In 2026, the domestic hardwood market has stabilized after the supply disruptions of 2022–2024, but pricing gaps between species have widened significantly. This guide covers the seven species that dominate serious furniture production in the U.S., with data drawn from the USDA Forest Products Laboratory *Wood Handbook* (FPL-GTR-282, 2021) and current wholesale pricing from the National Hardwood Lumber Association (NHLA) reports.

## The Core Data: Janka Hardness and Workability

Before choosing a species, understand what Janka hardness actually measures: the force in pounds required to embed a 0.444-inch steel ball to half its diameter into the face of the board. Higher numbers mean better dent resistance. The FPL database covers over 900 species; the furniture-relevant range runs from about 500 lbf (soft maple, some pine) to 2,200 lbf (hard hickory).

Workability is a separate metric. Hard, dense woods like hickory or hard maple are physically demanding to machine and hand-tool. Softer, more porous species like walnut or cherry cut cleanly and reward hand-tool work but dent more easily in service.

## Hard Maple: The Production Benchmark

Hard maple (*Acer saccharum*) sits at **1,450 lbf Janka**, making it the hardest common domestic furniture wood. It is the species used for bowling lanes, butcher blocks, and commercial flooring — applications that demand abuse resistance. In furniture, it is the production standard for kitchen cabinet boxes, workbench tops, and dining table surfaces intended for heavy daily use.

The finishing challenge is real. Hard maple's tight, uniform grain has almost no figure unless you select bird's-eye, curly, or tiger variants. Plain hard maple finishes to a sterile look that reads cheap unless the finish is handled carefully. It is also notoriously blotchy with oil-based stains — pre-conditioner is mandatory, or switch to a gel stain.

Wholesale pricing in 2026: **$5.50–$8.00 per board foot** for FAS (Firsts and Seconds) grade.

## Black Walnut: The Premium Domestic Standard

Black walnut (*Juglans nigra*) at **1,010 lbf Janka** is softer than maple or oak, but its combination of straight grain, chocolate-brown heartwood, and workability makes it the most desirable domestic furniture wood in 2026. Demand from custom furniture shops and high-end architectural millwork has kept walnut prices elevated since 2021.

Walnut finishes beautifully with oil, oil-varnish blends, or film finishes. The heartwood is naturally oily, which means adhesive bonds require wiped surfaces, but the oil also means the wood resists moisture penetration and checking better than most species without applied finish. Hand tools cut cleanly. Router work is precise. The wood is genuinely pleasurable to work.

The business reality: walnut commands a price premium from clients that no other domestic species can match. A walnut dining table signals quality in a way that maple does not, regardless of craftsmanship level.

Wholesale pricing in 2026: **$9.00–$15.00 per board foot** for FAS, with figured material reaching $20–$35.

## White Oak: The Structural and Aesthetic Workhorse

White oak (*Quercus alba*) at **1,360 lbf Janka** combines genuine hardness with the aesthetic flexibility that production shops need. The tyloses that seal white oak's pores (making it barrel-quality for liquid containment) also make it one of the most dimensionally stable domestic hardwoods. Quarter-sawn and rift-cut white oak's ray fleck pattern has become the defining visual language of Scandinavian-influenced American furniture since about 2018.

White oak stains predictably, cuts cleanly on the tablesaw and jointer, and accepts everything from water-based polyurethane to hard wax oil without fighting. For shops building modern furniture at volume, white oak is probably the single most commercially flexible species available.

Wholesale pricing in 2026: **$9.00–$13.00 per board foot** for rift-sawn FAS.

## Cherry: The Heirloom Material

American black cherry (*Prunus serotina*) at **950 lbf Janka** is softer than the other hardwoods in this guide, but cherry's patina behavior is unlike any other species. New cherry is a pale pinkish-brown. After 18–24 months of light exposure, it deepens to a warm reddish-brown that no stain can replicate. Antique cherry furniture from the 18th and 19th centuries — the pieces in museum collections — shows this patina at its full depth.

This has a business implication: clients who understand cherry return for it. The material self-markets across the lifespan of the piece. It also finishes with extreme ease. A single coat of Waterlox or oil-varnish blend brings cherry to gallery quality faster than any other species.

The dent risk is real. Cherry dents at kitchen-cabinet-door contact edges. Shops building cherry kitchen cabinets should specify frame-and-panel doors with slight setback edges, not flat slab doors that take contact hits directly.

Wholesale pricing in 2026: **$8.00–$12.00 per board foot** for FAS.

## Red Oak: The Builder's Grade

Red oak (*Quercus rubra*) at **1,290 lbf Janka** is the most widely available domestic hardwood in the U.S. lumber market. Its open grain and large pores make finishing more demanding than white oak, and the lack of tyloses means it is not suitable for any application where liquid resistance matters. However, its price point — often **$4.00–$6.00 per board foot** in FAS — and wide availability make it the standard for painted furniture, trim, and institutional millwork.

For natural-finish furniture, red oak reads as "builder grade" to educated buyers. For painted work, it is structurally sound and cost-effective.

## Ash: The Comeback Species

Ash (*Fraxinus* spp.) at **1,320 lbf Janka** almost disappeared from U.S. furniture production due to the emerald ash borer epidemic that eliminated an estimated 99% of ash trees in affected regions between 2002 and 2020 (USDA Animal and Plant Health Inspection Service, 2023 report). However, milled ash from salvaged urban trees has returned to market, and ash grown outside the borer's range is available from specialty suppliers.

Ash's open grain is similar to white oak but lighter in color, and it bends beautifully — it is the traditional material for Windsor chair spindles and bentwood work. For shops doing furniture with steam-bent components, ash is the domestic answer. Pricing varies widely due to supply uncertainty: **$6.00–$11.00 per board foot** when available.

## Soft Maple: The Budget Structural Option

Soft maple (*Acer rubrum*, *A. saccharinum*) at **700–950 lbf Janka** (species varies) is harder than most softwoods but softer than hard maple. It is frequently mislabeled at lumber yards. The important distinction: soft maple machines and finishes more like hard maple than like pine, but it dents under heavy use. It is acceptable for painted furniture, secondary surfaces (drawer sides, case backs), and furniture that will not be used on horizontal work surfaces.

Pricing in 2026: **$3.50–$5.50 per board foot** for FAS, making it the entry point for structural hardwood furniture.

## The 2026 Pricing Reality

The two dominant market forces in 2026 are: (1) walnut demand from high-end residential construction continuing to outpace supply from the mid-continent growing regions, and (2) white oak premium driven by Scandinavian-influenced design's sustained commercial dominance. Both species are 35–45% more expensive than their 2018 baselines.

For shops managing material cost across a mix of project types, a pragmatic portfolio approach works: white oak for volume production of modern pieces, walnut for premium commissions where the material cost is part of the price justification, and cherry for heirloom work where the patina argument closes client decisions.

The worst economic move in the current market is treating wood species as interchangeable and bidding projects without locking in material pricing. Hardwood prices at wholesale have shown 8–15% annual volatility since 2020. Quote material as a pass-through with a locked supplier price, or include a material escalation clause in contracts over 60-day delivery windows.
